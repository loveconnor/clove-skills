## 1. Purpose and operating principles

Marketing connects a real offer with people for whom it can create legitimate value. It should help those people recognize a relevant situation, understand the available choice, evaluate credible evidence, and act without deception or avoidable pressure.

Marketing strategy is not a calendar of promotional activity. It is a set of linked decisions about:

- which market and buying situations matter;
- which people or organizations the offer can serve well;
- which alternatives they would otherwise choose;
- which value the offer creates and what it costs or requires;
- why the value claim is credible;
- how the offer reaches, converts, activates, retains, and supports customers;
- which evidence will determine whether to continue, change, or stop.

### 1.1 Core principles

1. **Start with a customer decision, not a channel.** “Run paid social” is an execution choice. “Help operations leaders compare migration risk before budget approval” is a market task.
2. **Choose whom not to pursue.** A useful segment or ideal customer profile changes qualification, product, message, channel, service, and resource decisions.
3. **Position against the real alternative.** The competitor may be another product, internal work, an agency, a spreadsheet, delay, or doing nothing.
4. **Describe value through a mechanism.** Tie the relevant problem to a capability, observable outcome, proof, cost, and limitation.
5. **Separate internal hypotheses from public claims.** A strategic belief can guide a test; it cannot be published as fact without adequate support.
6. **Keep product truth and marketing truth identical.** Pricing, availability, permissions, implementation effort, security, results, and policies must agree across product, sales, support, contracts, feeds, ads, and public pages.
7. **Measure the whole path.** Delivery, attention opportunity, response, qualified action, revenue, contribution, retention, and brand effects answer different questions.
8. **Use incrementality for causal claims.** Attribution assigns credit under rules. A controlled comparison estimates what happened because of the marketing.
9. **Protect user agency.** Do not create false urgency, disguised advertising, hidden renewal, confirm-shaming, obstructive unsubscribe, or consent pressure.
10. **Preserve provenance and ownership.** Every consequential claim, audience definition, metric, creative asset, testimonial, and decision needs an owner and source.

### 1.2 What marketing evidence can establish

| Evidence | Can support | Cannot establish by itself |
|---|---|---|
| Interviews and sales calls | Language, context, objections, alternatives, mechanisms | Prevalence or market size |
| Product and CRM data | Recorded behavior and outcomes in the observed customer base | Motive, unserved demand, or causation without design |
| Survey | Estimated attitudes or reported behavior in the sampled population | Actual behavior or causation automatically |
| Campaign platform report | Delivery and attributed outcomes under platform definitions | Independent incremental impact |
| Randomized lift study | Causal effect in the tested population and period | Long-term or cross-market generalization automatically |
| Case study | A documented result under stated conditions | Typical results for all customers |
| Search and market data | Observable demand signals and competitive context | Complete demand or customer preference |
| Brand tracker | Movement in defined awareness, associations, or intent measures | Monetary brand value or sales causation by itself |

Use multiple evidence roles. A qualitative finding can reveal why a message fails; a representative survey can estimate how common the issue is; a campaign experiment can estimate whether a change caused an outcome.

## 2. Marketing evidence and source hierarchy

### 2.1 Use claim-source fit, not a universal ladder

| Question | Start with | Add |
|---|---|---|
| What can the product do now? | Versioned product, code, contract, operations, pricing, and policy truth | Reproducible test and support evidence |
| Who succeeds with it? | Cohort outcomes with a defined success measure | Interviews, implementation records, churn and support evidence |
| What problem language do buyers use? | Direct interviews, calls, search, support, and field observation | Survey or content analysis for distribution |
| How large is the market? | Official datasets and transparent bottom-up counts | Industry reports with methods and sensitivity ranges |
| Which message is understood? | Representative comprehension and task testing | Controlled behavioral test with quality guardrails |
| Did a campaign cause sales? | Randomized or credible quasi-experimental design | Marketing mix model calibrated with experiments |
| What does the law require? | Current enacted text, regulator, and controlling guidance | Qualified legal interpretation |
| What does a platform metric mean? | Current platform and measurement-standard documentation | Independent audit or reconciliation |

The closest source is not always independent. A company is authoritative about its current price; it is interested evidence about the superiority of its product.

### 2.2 Build a marketing truth packet

Before strategy, collect:

**Offer truth**

- capabilities and explicit limitations;
- price, packaging, contracts, availability, and eligibility;
- implementation, migration, maintenance, and support requirements;
- privacy, security, accessibility, legal, and operational constraints;
- reliable outcome and failure evidence.

**Market truth**

- category and substitute structure;
- customer situations, triggers, tasks, anxieties, and constraints;
- buying group, decision process, budget source, and time horizon;
- distribution, procurement, and switching barriers;
- demand, growth, regulation, seasonality, and geography;
- competitive claims, evidence, pricing basis, and channel presence.

**Business truth**

- capacity, margin, service cost, cash timing, and payback tolerance;
- sales, implementation, support, and inventory readiness;
- acquisition and retention economics by cohort;
- acceptable risk and guardrail thresholds;
- owners for decisions, claims, and maintenance.

Unknowns remain hypotheses. Do not convert a desired market, invented customer quote, target result, or speculative feature into public content.

### 2.3 Preserve provenance

For each material finding, record:

- atomic claim or observation;
- source, creator, date, version, and exact locator;
- population, method, sample, field dates, and exclusions;
- whether it is direct evidence, calculation, interpretation, or recommendation;
- funding, commercial interest, and evidence lineage;
- conflicting evidence and transfer limits;
- confidence and review trigger.

Use the [research methods guide](../../research/references/research.md) for source records, claim-evidence ledgers, contradiction handling, and synthesis.

### 2.4 Search for disconfirmation

Search deliberately for:

- customers who failed, churned, returned, or never activated;
- deals lost to inaction or an indirect alternative;
- implementations that exceeded cost or time;
- negative reviews and support themes;
- competitor advantages the team would rather ignore;
- segments with weak margin or high service burden;
- campaign null results and harm signals;
- evidence that the proposed category or message is misunderstood;
- legal, platform, accessibility, or privacy constraints.

A strategy that only explains wins is not ready.

## 3. Market diagnosis and opportunity research

### 3.1 Frame the decision

Write a market decision brief:

```markdown
<owner> must decide whether and how to enter, defend, or expand in <market>
for <people or organizations> in <geography/time>.
The decision should create <customer outcome> and <business outcome>
without exceeding <cost, capacity, risk, or ethical constraints>.
```

Specify what evidence would change the decision. Otherwise, market research can become an exercise in justifying an already preferred launch.

### 3.2 Define the market through a need and exchange

An industry code is useful for counting suppliers, not sufficient to define a customer market. The U.S. [North American Industry Classification System](https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/economic-census/year/2022/guidance/understanding-naics.html) groups establishments by similar production processes. Customer alternatives can cross those industry boundaries.

Define:

- person or organization with the need;
- triggering situation;
- outcome sought;
- available alternatives;
- ability and authority to act;
- exchange of money, time, data, effort, risk, or attention;
- geographic, regulatory, technical, and temporal boundary.

### 3.3 Use complementary research methods

| Need | Useful method | Important limit |
|---|---|---|
| Discover situations and language | Interviews, field observation, call analysis | Not prevalence |
| Estimate population and firms | Census, administrative, registry, and industry data | Classification may not equal demand |
| Estimate needs or attitudes | Probability or carefully sampled survey | Self-report, nonresponse, question effects |
| Observe current behavior | Product, web, retail, CRM, and transaction data | Only observed population and system |
| Understand alternatives | Win/loss, churn, search results, procurement and competitive research | Recall and stakeholder bias |
| Test willingness to act | Landing-page, offer, sales, pilot, or field experiment | Test design and brand effects matter |
| Estimate causal demand | Randomized price, channel, or market experiment where appropriate | Ethics, interference, and external validity |

The U.S. Small Business Administration distinguishes secondary sources for general quantitative context from direct customer research for business-specific questions ([market research and competitive analysis](https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/plan-your-business/market-research-competitive-analysis)). The [Census Business Builder](https://www.census.gov/data/data-tools/cbb.html) provides official economic and demographic data for U.S. geographic and industry analysis.

### 3.4 Separate market facts from forecasts

**Observed fact:** number of establishments in a defined code and geography.

**Estimated fact:** weighted survey estimate with an interval.

**Forecast:** future outcome under assumptions.

**Target:** what the business aims to achieve.

Do not label a target “SOM” or a forecast “current demand” without the model.

### 3.5 Build TAM, SAM, and SOM from explicit units

- **Total addressable market (TAM):** all demand under the broad stated market definition.
- **Serviceable available market (SAM):** demand the actual offer can serve under current geography, eligibility, product, capacity, and channel constraints.
- **Serviceable obtainable market (SOM):** a scenario for realistically captured demand over a defined horizon, not a fact.

Bottom-up example:

```text
eligible organizations
× relevant buying-event rate per year
× units per buying event
× average net price
= annual serviceable demand under stated assumptions
```

Show low, base, and high scenarios. Test sensitivity to eligibility, event frequency, price, adoption, capacity, churn, and competitive response. Never present “1% of a huge category” as an obtainable market without a mechanism.

### 3.6 Diagnose market structure

Map:

- direct products with the same promised outcome;
- adjacent tools used in the same workflow;
- internal, manual, or service alternatives;
- doing nothing or delaying;
- distribution and gatekeepers;
- buyers, users, approvers, blockers, and beneficiaries;
- complementors and dependencies;
- switching, learning, integration, contractual, and social costs;
- regulation and standards;
- category language and ambiguity.

Competitive analysis should include what each alternative does better and for whom. A feature checklist rarely captures trust, fit, switching cost, distribution, or service quality.

### 3.7 Use surveys transparently

[AAPOR's survey best practices](https://aapor.org/standards-and-ethics/best-practices/) emphasize question fit, sampling, questionnaire design, analysis, and transparent reporting. For published market surveys, preserve:

- target population and sampling frame;
- recruitment and field dates;
- mode, language, and incentive;
- sample size and completion rate;
- full question wording, options, order, and randomization;
- weighting and design effect;
- margin of sampling error when probability sampling supports it;
- nonresponse, coverage, and measurement limits;
- sponsor and research provider;
- analysis and exclusions.

A large opt-in survey can still be biased. A margin of sampling error does not capture coverage, nonresponse, question, or weighting error.

## 4. Segmentation and ideal customer profiles

Segmentation divides a heterogeneous market into groups that differ in ways that should change strategy. The concept has a long research history; Wendell Smith's foundational article treated product differentiation and market segmentation as alternative strategies ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/002224295602100102)).

### 4.1 Segment on differences that change action

Useful dimensions include:

- triggering situation and job;
- desired outcome and consequence of failure;
- current alternative and switching barrier;
- use frequency, volume, complexity, or maturity;
- required capability, integration, compliance, or service;
- economic value and cost to serve;
- buying process, authority, and procurement path;
- geography, language, regulation, or availability;
- channel accessibility and evidence needs;
- lifecycle state and observed behavior.

Demographics, firmographics, and technographics can help locate a segment. They are rarely sufficient explanations for why it buys.

### 4.2 Evaluate segment quality

A segment should be:

- **meaningfully different:** needs or response differ;
- **identifiable:** membership can be assessed without invasive inference;
- **measurable enough:** size, value, or behavior can be estimated honestly;
- **reachable:** channels and permissions exist;
- **serviceable:** the product and operation can deliver the promise;
- **economically sustainable:** price, margin, retention, and cost to serve fit;
- **stable enough:** useful over the decision horizon;
- **ethical and lawful:** targeting does not exploit vulnerability or prohibited attributes;
- **actionable:** offer, product, proof, channel, or service changes.

If every segment receives the same offer and message, the segmentation may be descriptive rather than strategic.

### 4.3 Define an ICP as a fit hypothesis

An ideal customer profile (ICP) describes the type of customer for whom the offer is most likely to create durable value while the business can serve them sustainably. It is not merely the largest customer, easiest lead, or person the company wants to impress.

For B2B, distinguish:

- **account ICP:** organization-level fit;
- **buying group:** people who initiate, evaluate, approve, block, purchase, implement, and use;
- **use-case profile:** situation and workflow where value occurs;
- **contact persona:** role-specific information needs and objections.

For B2C, the equivalent often combines person/household eligibility, buying situation, need, constraints, channel, and value potential.

### 4.4 ICP fields

| Field | Definition |
|---|---|
| Trigger | Event that makes change relevant now |
| Job and outcome | Progress the customer needs |
| Current alternative | What they do today |
| Pain/consequence | Cost or risk of the current state |
| Required capabilities | Non-negotiable product and service fit |
| Readiness | Authority, budget, data, skill, process, and timing |
| Value potential | Observable outcome and economic significance |
| Cost to serve | Sales, onboarding, integration, support, and risk burden |
| Evidence need | Proof required to believe and approve |
| Buying group | Champion, user, approver, procurement, security, blocker |
| Disqualifiers | Conditions that predict poor fit or harm |
| Observable signals | Ethical, lawful signals available before purchase |
| Confidence | Evidence and limitations behind the profile |

### 4.5 Build ICPs from outcomes, including failures

Use:

- activation and retained-value cohorts;
- contribution margin and support cost;
- implementation duration and failure;
- renewals, expansion, contraction, and churn;
- sales cycle, loss reason, and discounting;
- customer interviews and field observation;
- referrals and advocacy with verified provenance;
- customers who should not have been sold the product.

Avoid survivorship bias. Studying only current enthusiastic customers removes failed implementations, silent churn, and prospects excluded by the existing motion.

### 4.6 Score transparently

An ICP score can support prioritization if its logic is visible:

```text
fit score = required eligibility gate
          + weighted product fit
          + weighted value potential
          + weighted readiness
          - service and risk burden
```

Do not imply scientific precision. Validate whether the score predicts later activation, retained value, and sustainable economics. Audit it for proxy discrimination and self-fulfilling exclusion.

### 4.7 Maintain a negative ICP

Record people or organizations the offer should not target because of:

- missing required capability or integration;
- need the product cannot meet;
- unacceptable safety, privacy, compliance, or accessibility risk;
- inability to achieve the promised outcome;
- service requirement the business cannot support;
- harmful economics for either party;
- conflict with product values or legal eligibility.

Disqualification protects trust. Do not use marketing to push a knowingly poor-fit customer through conversion.

### 4.8 ICPs are not personas

An evidence-based persona can communicate role, context, knowledge, goals, and decisions. Decorative names, stock photos, hobbies, and invented quotes add little. Keep the ICP operational; use role-specific message profiles only where information needs genuinely differ.

## 5. Positioning

Positioning defines the meaning the offer should hold, relative to alternatives, in a specific buying situation. It guides product, category, message, proof, price, channel, and sales choices. It is not the public tagline.

### 5.1 Positioning components

1. **Priority situation:** when the offer becomes relevant.
2. **Target:** who has the situation, authority, need, and fit.
3. **Frame of reference:** category or comparison that helps people understand the choice.
4. **Alternative:** what they would otherwise choose.
5. **Problem with the alternative:** consequential gap, not generic frustration.
6. **Differentiated value:** outcome the offer enables better for this target.
7. **Mechanism:** capabilities, process, model, distribution, or experience that creates the value.
8. **Proof:** evidence appropriate to the claim.
9. **Tradeoff:** what the offer does not optimize and for whom it is not best.

### 5.2 Position relative to a buying situation

Weak:

> The all-in-one AI-powered platform for modern teams.

Stronger internal position:

> For regulated support teams that must review every generated answer before publication, the product is a governed drafting workspace that connects each claim to approved sources and review status. Unlike a general chat tool, it preserves source lineage and blocks unreviewed publishing. It requires a maintained source library and is not intended for fully autonomous response.

The stronger version identifies target, situation, category, alternative, mechanism, evidence expectation, and sacrifice.

### 5.3 Choose the category deliberately

A category frame can:

- make the product quickly recognizable;
- activate expected comparison criteria;
- determine competitors and budget source;
- set price expectations;
- help or hinder discovery.

Creating a new category imposes education cost. It is warranted only when existing categories produce materially wrong expectations and the organization can define, demonstrate, and sustain the new frame.

Test whether people can explain:

- what it is;
- when they would use it;
- what it replaces;
- how it differs;
- why the difference matters.

### 5.4 Find differentiation that survives scrutiny

Potential differentiation sources:

- superior outcome in a defined condition;
- distinctive mechanism or capability;
- integration into a critical workflow;
- distribution, availability, speed, or service model;
- lower total burden, risk, or time;
- specialized evidence, data, expertise, or community;
- experience quality or accessibility;
- business model, guarantee, or accountability;
- brand associations and distinctive assets.

“Easy,” “secure,” “innovative,” and “better” require definitions and evidence. A feature competitors can copy may still matter if the complete system, proof, or distribution is hard to replicate.

### 5.5 Compare all alternatives honestly

For each alternative, record:

- situation in which it is the better choice;
- strengths and limitations;
- total financial and operational cost;
- switching and implementation burden;
- trust and risk profile;
- evidence source and date;
- likely competitive response.

Do not position by inventing a weak competitor. The status quo often wins because it is familiar and already paid for.

### 5.6 Positioning research methods

- category and language interviews;
- win/loss and no-decision analysis;
- sales and support call coding;
- competitive experience and claim audits;
- search-result and query-intent research;
- switching and workflow observation;
- message comprehension and sorting;
- survey estimation after qualitative vocabulary is stable;
- controlled landing-page, offer, or sales tests;
- cohort outcomes by target segment.

Preference for a sentence is weak evidence. Test accurate interpretation, relevance, distinctiveness, credibility, qualified action, and later outcome.

### 5.7 Positioning statement template

```markdown
For <priority target> when <buying situation>,
<offer> is a <frame of reference>
that enables <specific valued outcome>
because <mechanism and evidence>.
Unlike <real alternative>, it <decision-relevant difference>.
It is not intended for <boundary or tradeoff>.
```

Keep this internal. Public copy should express the position in language suited to each context.

## 6. Messaging and proof architecture

Messaging translates position into an ordered set of claims, explanations, proof, and actions for a particular audience and moment.

### 6.1 Build a message hierarchy

| Layer | Question answered |
|---|---|
| Category/orientation | What is this? |
| Relevance | Is it for a situation like mine? |
| Outcome | What progress can it help me make? |
| Differentiation | Why choose it over my alternative? |
| Mechanism | How does it create that result? |
| Proof | Why should I believe this? |
| Cost and tradeoff | What will it require from me? |
| Objection | What could prevent or delay the decision? |
| Action | What is the appropriate next step? |

Do not force every layer into a hero section. Sequence them according to audience knowledge, risk, and buying stage.

### 6.2 Use a claim-mechanism-proof unit

```text
Claim: the bounded outcome or capability.
Mechanism: how the offer creates it.
Proof: evidence that supports the claim.
Boundary: who, when, version, and limitation.
Action: next step consistent with the evidence.
```

Example:

> Import approved terminology from your design system, then flag inconsistent product strings before review. In our documented test set, version 4.2 identified 186 of 200 seeded term violations. It did not evaluate whether the approved term was itself correct. Review the methodology and test it on a sample repository.

### 6.3 Adapt by buying-group role

Keep the core position stable while changing information depth:

- **User:** workflow, effort, control, recovery, and day-to-day outcome.
- **Champion:** organizational value, rollout, internal proof, and stakeholder support.
- **Economic buyer:** total cost, return, risk, and alternatives.
- **Technical evaluator:** architecture, integration, security, performance, and limitations.
- **Procurement/legal:** terms, data handling, liability, accessibility, and supplier evidence.
- **Executive:** strategic consequence, evidence, resource commitment, and accountability.

Do not tell incompatible stories to different stakeholders. Tailoring changes emphasis, not facts.

### 6.4 Distinguish message types

- **Fact:** directly verifiable current state.
- **Capability:** what the offer can do under defined conditions.
- **Outcome:** observed or expected change for a customer.
- **Comparative claim:** difference from a named or defined alternative.
- **Estimate:** calculation under explicit inputs.
- **Forecast:** future result under assumptions.
- **Opinion:** attributed judgment.
- **Testimonial:** one person's genuine experience, not general substantiation.
- **Promise or guarantee:** commitment with enforceable conditions and remedy.

Label and substantiate each appropriately.

### 6.5 Write for recognition and decision

- use the audience's verified vocabulary;
- keep one term per concept;
- lead with a concrete situation or outcome;
- explain unfamiliar category language;
- state costs, requirements, limits, and material conditions before commitment;
- make numbers carry baseline, denominator, period, and method;
- avoid generic claims that any competitor could make;
- preserve translation and accessibility;
- use evidence near the claim.

Clear marketing can still be distinctive. Distinctiveness comes from a specific observation, point of view, proof, verbal system, imagery, or experience—not from hiding meaning.

### 6.6 Test the message in layers

1. **Interpretation:** What do people think it says?
2. **Relevance:** Do intended customers recognize their situation?
3. **Differentiation:** Can they explain why it differs from the real alternative?
4. **Credibility:** Which part do they believe or doubt, and why?
5. **Recall:** What remains later without prompting?
6. **Decision:** Does it support an informed next step?
7. **Behavior:** Does it improve qualified action with guardrails?
8. **Outcome:** Do acquired customers activate, retain, and succeed?

Clicks can increase because a message is vague or sensational. Pair response with qualification, comprehension, complaint, cancellation, and retained-value measures.

## 7. Offer, pricing, and conversion design

### 7.1 Treat the offer as a complete exchange

The offer includes:

- product or service;
- price and billing basis;
- contract, renewal, cancellation, and refund;
- implementation and customer effort;
- risk, data, and permission exchange;
- service, warranty, and support;
- proof and guarantee;
- availability, deadline, and capacity;
- next action.

A good message cannot rescue an unsuitable or obscured exchange.

### 7.2 Price evidence

Test price with methods suited to the decision:

- actual transaction and discount behavior;
- controlled offer or market tests where ethical and lawful;
- win/loss and procurement evidence;
- price sensitivity surveys used as stated-preference evidence;
- total-cost comparison;
- cohort margin, retention, and support burden;
- channel and partner economics.

Willingness stated in an interview is not willingness demonstrated in purchase. A discount can raise conversion while reducing trust, margin, or later retention.

### 7.3 Make urgency factual

Urgency must come from a real constraint:

- inventory or service capacity;
- registration or legal deadline;
- actual offer end;
- time-sensitive external event;
- customer consequence of delay.

Do not reset countdowns, invent low stock, imply competition that does not exist, or call an evergreen price a limited offer.

### 7.4 Align landing and acquisition promises

The landing experience should preserve:

- audience and situation;
- offer and price;
- claim scope and evidence;
- visual and verbal identity;
- expected next step;
- tracking and consent state.

Message mismatch wastes spend and damages trust. Do not use a broad claim in an ad and narrow it materially only after the click.

### 7.5 Design the conversion path for agency

- state what happens after the action;
- ask only for necessary information;
- distinguish marketing consent from service requirements;
- preserve input and support recovery;
- give comparison and evidence before commitment;
- make decline, cancel, and unsubscribe understandable;
- confirm the actual outcome and next step;
- never simulate success or availability.

## 8. Go-to-market strategy

Go-to-market (GTM) strategy defines how an offer reaches a market, creates and captures value, and becomes operationally supportable. A launch is one milestone inside GTM.

### 8.1 GTM decision system

Define:

1. target market and ICP;
2. priority buying situations and positioning;
3. offer, packaging, pricing, and commercial model;
4. acquisition and distribution channels;
5. sales, self-service, partner, or hybrid motion;
6. onboarding, implementation, service, and success model;
7. proof and risk reduction;
8. demand generation and lifecycle plan;
9. capacity, unit economics, and operating constraints;
10. measurement, learning agenda, and stop/scale rules.

### 8.2 Choose motion from buying reality

| Motion | Fits when | Main risk |
|---|---|---|
| Self-service/product-led | Value can be reached safely with low assistance | Sign-up mistaken for value; poor-fit volume |
| Sales-led | Decision is complex, high-risk, multi-party, or configurable | Expensive acquisition and promise inconsistency |
| Partner/channel-led | Trusted access, implementation, or local presence matters | Loss of message, data, and experience control |
| Community/developer-led | Adoption depends on practice, ecosystem, or technical trust | Community treated as a lead list |
| Marketplace/platform-led | Buyers already procure through a platform | Platform dependency and margin pressure |
| Hybrid | Different stages need different help | Handoffs, ownership, and attribution conflict |

Do not choose a motion because it is fashionable. Match purchase risk, contract value, integration, time-to-value, and buyer preference.

### 8.3 Establish readiness gates

**Product**

- intended task works under realistic conditions;
- target segment can achieve first value;
- accessibility, privacy, security, performance, and reliability meet the promise;
- pricing, entitlements, flags, and limits are correct.

**Commercial**

- positioning, claims, evidence, offer, and terms are approved;
- CRM, routing, qualification, quoting, and billing work;
- sales and partners can explain fit and disqualify poor fit.

**Operational**

- inventory, capacity, implementation, support, incident, cancellation, and refunds are ready;
- analytics and quality monitors are live;
- owner, rollback, and escalation exist.

**Market**

- channel access and audience permission exist;
- content and proof cover the decision journey;
- legal and platform reviews are complete;
- response and failure thresholds are defined.

### 8.4 Sequence learning before scale

1. Validate product truth and customer outcome.
2. Validate ICP and disqualification.
3. Validate positioning comprehension.
4. Validate offer and channel response with a bounded audience.
5. Validate activation, support, and early retention.
6. Validate economics and incremental effect.
7. Expand spend, markets, channels, and segments gradually.

Do not scale lead generation into a broken onboarding or unsupported service.

### 8.5 Launch tiers

Use tiers based on consequence and coordination, not executive attention.

| Tier | Typical scope | Required coordination |
|---|---|---|
| Quiet release | Existing audience, reversible, low support impact | Product truth, documentation, analytics, support note |
| Targeted launch | Specific ICP or market, bounded spend | Full GTM brief, enablement, lifecycle, measurement, incident plan |
| Major launch | New product/category/market or irreversible public claim | Executive decision, legal, operations, partners, brand, PR, scale and rollback plan |

### 8.6 GTM failure modes

- launching to everyone before fit is known;
- confusing distribution with demand;
- measuring leads while activation fails;
- hiding implementation burden;
- using a new category without education budget;
- acquiring customers the product should reject;
- scaling before support and capacity;
- changing product, price, audience, and creative at once without a learning design;
- declaring launch success from impressions or press coverage.

## 9. Campaign strategy and execution

A campaign is a coordinated, time-bounded intervention intended to change a defined audience outcome. Always-on programs still need distinct objectives, audiences, creative systems, and measurement periods.

### 9.1 Campaign brief

Specify:

- business and customer objective;
- priority audience and eligibility;
- buying situation and journey stage;
- behavior or belief to change;
- single primary proposition;
- supporting messages and proof;
- offer and action;
- creative idea and distinctive assets;
- channel role and media plan;
- geography, dates, budget, reach, and frequency plan;
- landing and lifecycle handoff;
- primary, diagnostic, guardrail, and quality metrics;
- experiment or comparison design;
- claim, privacy, legal, accessibility, brand, and platform approval;
- owner, escalation, and stop rules.

### 9.2 Give each channel a role

| Role | Channel examples | Evidence to inspect |
|---|---|---|
| Create mental availability | Broad-reach video, audio, out-of-home, sponsorship | Reach, frequency, brand lift, search demand, long-term outcomes |
| Capture active demand | Search, marketplaces, comparison, sales response | Qualified response, conversion, incrementality, marginal cost |
| Educate and validate | Content, events, webinars, documentation, email | Comprehension, qualified progression, influenced—not automatically caused—revenue |
| Activate | Onboarding, in-product, implementation, success | First value, time, error, retained task success |
| Retain or expand | Service, education, lifecycle, account work | Value recurrence, renewal, expansion, complaint and opt-out |
| Earn trust and distribution | PR, community, partners, advocates | Accurate coverage, referral, reputation, qualified action |

Paid, owned, earned, partner, sales, retail, and product surfaces interact. Avoid assigning one result to all of them.

### 9.3 Distinguish delivery metrics

- **Served impression:** ad delivery counted under a defined standard.
- **Viewable impression:** met a viewability definition; it is an opportunity to see, not proof of attention.
- **Reach:** deduplicated people or devices exposed under the measurement method.
- **Frequency:** exposures per reached unit under that method.
- **Click:** defined interaction, subject to invalid traffic and accidental response.
- **Completed view:** media-specific completion, not comprehension.
- **Attention measure:** method-specific proxy; no universal currency.

IAB and MRC guidance defines impression, click, viewability, and invalid-traffic practices ([standards catalog](https://www.iab.com/guidelines/), [click guidelines](https://www.iab.com/guidelines/click-measurement-guidelines/), [viewable impression guidelines](https://www.iab.com/guidelines/mrc-viewable-impression-guidelines/), [invalid traffic addendum](https://www.iab.com/guidelines/mrc-invalid-traffic-ivt-detection-and-filtration-guidelines-addendum/)). Record which standard and vendor method a report uses.

### 9.4 Plan reach and frequency rather than spend alone

Report:

- eligible audience definition;
- estimated and measured deduplicated reach;
- frequency distribution, not only average;
- platform and cross-platform duplication;
- saturation, wear-out, and response by exposure;
- excluded inventory and invalid traffic;
- incremental reach from each channel;
- privacy and identity limitations.

The [WFA cross-media framework](https://wfanet.org/l/library/download/urn%3Auuid%3Aed583162-f638-4dd6-87b8-f831c4550761/the%2Bhalo%2Bcross-media%2Bmeasurement%2Bframework.pdf) emphasizes comparable, deduplicated reach and frequency with outcome measurement. Platform totals should not be simply added as people.

### 9.5 Build a creative system

Separate:

- stable position and proposition;
- creative territory or organizing idea;
- distinctive brand assets;
- audience/context variant;
- format and placement adaptation;
- individual execution.

Test meaningful differences. Changing a button color while the proposition and evidence are unvalidated wastes learning capacity.

### 9.6 Test creative quality

Evaluate:

- correct brand identification;
- accurate message takeaway;
- relevance to intended buying situation;
- distinctiveness from competitors;
- credibility and evidence comprehension;
- emotional and cultural interpretation;
- accessibility across audio, visual, motion, captions, contrast, and language;
- qualified action;
- annoyance, confusion, complaint, hide, and opt-out;
- performance across placements without misleading crops or missing disclosure.

### 9.7 Use a campaign taxonomy

Maintain stable identifiers for:

```text
campaign
initiative
audience
market
channel
publisher/platform
placement
creative concept
creative execution
offer
landing destination
experiment and variant
```

UTM parameters and platform names should map to this controlled taxonomy. Never put personal data or sensitive audience labels in URLs.

### 9.8 Campaign review cadence

**Before launch:** truth, setup, readiness, baseline, power, and stop rules.

**Early:** delivery, broken paths, invalid traffic, spend, consent, severe guardrails.

**In flight:** reach/frequency, quality, response, audience mix, creative, lift readiness, and operational burden.

**After:** mature conversions, incrementality, brand effects, cohort quality, economics, contradictions, and reusable learning.

Do not optimize to an early proxy that changes the audience or destroys the planned experiment.

## 10. Lifecycle marketing

Lifecycle marketing provides relevant communication and support based on a person's relationship, needs, and product state. It should not be a fixed sequence of messages sent because time elapsed.

### 10.1 Model states and transitions

Possible states:

```text
unaware / category entry
-> exploring
-> evaluating
-> committed
-> onboarding
-> first value
-> recurring value
-> expanding
-> at risk
-> lapsed
-> reactivated or closed
```

Real journeys loop, pause, use multiple channels, and include several people. Define transitions through observable events and domain states, not arbitrary lead-score labels.

### 10.2 Lifecycle specification

For each program, define:

- eligible state and exclusions;
- user need and legitimate purpose;
- trigger and evidence source;
- message and promised value;
- channel and permission;
- timing, frequency, priority, and suppression;
- exit and success event;
- failure, complaint, unsubscribe, and escalation;
- experiment and guardrails;
- owner and expiry.

### 10.3 Separate service from promotion

Transactional or relationship messages confirm or operate an existing agreement. Promotional messages encourage additional commercial action. Mixing them can change user interpretation and legal treatment.

The U.S. FTC's [CAN-SPAM guide](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business) explains requirements for commercial email and distinguishes transactional/relationship content from commercial content. UK rules differ; the ICO's [current electronic-mail marketing guidance](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/direct-marketing-and-privacy-and-electronic-communications/guidance-on-direct-marketing-using-electronic-mail/) was updated in 2026. Verify applicable jurisdiction, recipient, channel, relationship, and consent rules.

### 10.4 Consent and preference design

- explain channel, purpose, sender, and expected frequency;
- make choice freely given where consent is used;
- keep marketing preference separate from required service terms;
- store source, text, timestamp, jurisdiction, and version;
- make withdrawal simple and prompt;
- propagate suppression to vendors, campaigns, and exports;
- support channel and topic preferences where operationally honest;
- do not treat silence, inactivity, or dark-pattern acceptance as meaningful preference;
- retain only the data needed for the stated purpose.

The ICO's guidance emphasizes data-protection-by-design and respecting people's preferences ([direct marketing guidance](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/direct-marketing-and-privacy-and-electronic-communications/direct-marketing-guidance/)).

### 10.5 Onboarding and activation

Lifecycle messages should help the customer reach a real value state:

- restate the intended outcome;
- surface prerequisites and blockers;
- use event-based guidance at the point of need;
- coordinate in-product, email, sales, implementation, and support;
- preserve control and avoid notification overload;
- distinguish education from upsell;
- stop the sequence after success, disqualification, closure, or support escalation.

Measure first value, time, error, recovery, and later retained value. Email opens are not activation.

### 10.6 Retention, expansion, and risk

Retention communication should respond to genuine need:

- new capability relevant to current use;
- renewal facts and material changes;
- underused value with a clear, non-blaming path;
- support, education, and success planning;
- risk or outage communication;
- expansion only when fit and value support it.

Do not use friction, hidden cancellation, or anxiety to manufacture retention. Churn prevented by obstruction is not customer value.

### 10.7 Win-back and reactivation

Define why reactivation could help the person now. Suppress:

- unresolved complaints;
- closed or deleted accounts;
- prior explicit refusal;
- disqualified fit;
- safety, fraud, legal, or support restrictions;
- recipients beyond permitted retention or contact windows.

Measure durable reactivation and value, not one click or discounted return.

### 10.8 Lifecycle guardrails

- unsubscribe and complaint;
- spam placement and deliverability;
- notification disablement;
- support contact and confusion;
- duplicate or conflicting messages;
- cancellation and refund;
- privacy request and consent failure;
- vulnerable-person harm;
- accessibility and language failure;
- long-term retention quality.

## 11. Brand strategy and measurement

A brand is not only its visual identity. It is a set of identifiers, associations, expectations, memories, experiences, and legally or socially recognized relationships connected to an entity or offer.

### 11.1 Separate brand constructs

- **Identity:** chosen name, verbal system, visual system, behavior, and assets.
- **Awareness:** recognition or recall under a defined question.
- **Mental availability:** likelihood of coming to mind in relevant buying situations.
- **Associations/image:** meanings linked to the brand.
- **Consideration:** inclusion in a stated choice set.
- **Preference/intention:** reported inclination under conditions.
- **Experience:** product and service encounters.
- **Reputation:** external judgments and evidence.
- **Behavioral strength:** acquisition, retention, price, distribution, and choice outcomes.
- **Brand valuation:** monetary estimation under a defined financial method.

Keller's foundational customer-based brand equity model distinguishes awareness and brand image/associations ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/002224299305700101)). ISO separately treats integrated brand evaluation and monetary brand valuation: [ISO 20671-1:2021](https://www.iso.org/standard/81739.html) provides principles for brand evaluation, while ISO 10668 addresses monetary valuation.

### 11.2 Brand measurement framework

| Layer | Measures | Important caveat |
|---|---|---|
| Inputs | Spend, reach, frequency, distribution, experience investment | Input is not effect |
| Memory | Unaided/aided awareness, category cues, asset recognition | Survey and cue design change results |
| Meaning | Associations, differentiation, trust, relevance | Stated belief, not behavior automatically |
| Choice | Consideration, preference, intent, trial | Intention differs from purchase |
| Experience | Satisfaction, task success, quality, support, complaint | Current customers are selected population |
| Behavior | Penetration, repeat, retention, price realization, referrals | Many non-brand factors contribute |
| Financial | Revenue, contribution, cash flow, valuation | Attribution and forecasting assumptions matter |
| Reputation | Independent coverage, reviews, citations, employer/partner trust | Volume and sentiment methods can be biased |

No single “brand score” should hide these mechanisms.

### 11.3 Track awareness consistently

**Unaided recall:** ask which brands come to mind without presenting names.

**Aided awareness:** ask recognition after showing a controlled list.

Do not compare aided to unaided awareness. Preserve:

- question wording and order;
- category and buying-situation cue;
- list composition and rotation;
- spelling and coding rules;
- sample, mode, weighting, geography, and field dates;
- competitor set;
- confidence interval and change threshold.

Awareness alone is not positive. People can recognize a brand for failure or controversy.

### 11.4 Measure associations and distinctiveness

For associations:

- use open and closed measures;
- distinguish brand ownership from category-common attributes;
- include negative and “none/don't know” responses;
- track strength, uniqueness, and relevance separately;
- test whether the association appears in real choice contexts.

For distinctive assets:

- test recognition without the brand name where appropriate;
- test correct brand linkage and competitor confusion;
- test across formats, sizes, motion, audio, accessibility, and markets;
- record actual use consistency;
- do not treat designer preference as asset strength.

### 11.5 Use stable brand trackers

Maintain:

- target population and sampling frame;
- sample size and weighting;
- recurring field cadence and seasonal controls;
- stable core questions and documented changes;
- exposure and customer-status variables;
- competitor and category set;
- confidence intervals and multiple-comparison awareness;
- raw trends, not only index scores;
- qualitative research to interpret movement.

When methodology changes, overlap old and new measures where possible. Do not splice incompatible series silently.

### 11.6 Brand lift studies

A brand lift study compares survey outcomes between exposed or eligible treatment and control populations. Google describes Brand Lift measures such as recall, awareness, association, and consideration, while its lift framework uses treatment/control comparisons ([About lift studies](https://support.google.com/google-ads/answer/16104408), [About Brand Lift](https://support.google.com/google-ads/answer/9049825)).

Check:

- assignment and exposure method;
- contamination and cross-channel exposure;
- eligible population and platform coverage;
- survey question, competitor list, and order;
- response and nonresponse bias;
- sample size, power, and multiple outcomes;
- absolute difference and interval;
- novelty and persistence;
- whether the vendor modeled any result.

Platform brand lift is useful evidence for that platform and design, not a complete brand valuation.

### 11.7 Behavioral brand signals

Possible supporting signals:

- branded search demand;
- direct or known-brand navigation;
- organic mentions and citations;
- share of category buyers;
- new-customer penetration;
- repeat or retention;
- price realization and discount dependence;
- distribution and availability;
- referral and advocacy;
- recruiting and partner response.

Each has confounders. Search demand can move with PR, controversy, season, distribution, and performance media. “Share of search” is a proxy, not brand equity or guaranteed future share.

### 11.8 Brand health synthesis

Report:

1. market and customer outcome;
2. memory and association movement;
3. experience and reputation evidence;
4. behavioral and financial movement;
5. campaign and distribution inputs;
6. plausible mechanisms and contradictions;
7. uncertainty and actions.

Do not interpret a two-point tracker change without its interval, sample, trend, and practical threshold.

## 12. Marketing economics and performance metrics

Use the [product analytics guide](../../analytics/references/research.md) for event, funnel, cohort, experiment, and interpretation rules.

### 12.1 Metric roles

- **Business outcome:** contribution, retained customers, qualified service outcome.
- **Marketing outcome:** incremental acquisition, demand, or brand movement.
- **Funnel diagnostic:** reach, response, qualification, progression, activation.
- **Channel delivery:** impression, viewability, click, cost.
- **Guardrail:** complaint, refund, churn, support, privacy, accessibility, margin.
- **Quality:** tracking coverage, invalid traffic, identity, freshness, reconciliation.

Do not use a delivery metric as the business objective.

### 12.2 Core definitions

```text
response rate = qualified responses / eligible delivered or reached units

conversion rate = defined outcomes / defined eligible opportunities

customer acquisition cost (CAC) = acquisition costs / newly acquired customers

incremental CAC = incremental campaign cost / incremental new customers

return on ad spend (ROAS) = attributed revenue / ad spend

incremental ROAS = incremental contribution or revenue / incremental spend
```

State whether acquisition cost includes media, agency, creative, sales, technology, discounts, and overhead. A narrow paid-media CAC should not be compared with a fully loaded sales-and-marketing CAC.

### 12.3 Use contribution, not revenue alone

Where possible:

```text
contribution = net revenue
             - cost of goods/service delivery
             - variable support and fulfillment
             - refunds, returns, credits, and payment costs
```

Exact accounting belongs to finance. Marketing should not call revenue return when the campaign acquires unprofitable or quickly churning customers.

### 12.4 Cohort LTV

Customer lifetime value depends on horizon, margin, retention, expansion, cost, and discounting. Report:

- customer and cohort definition;
- gross or contribution basis;
- observed versus forecast period;
- acquisition date and channel;
- refunds, churn, and expansion;
- service and support cost;
- discount rate if used;
- model and sensitivity range.

Avoid one universal LTV:CAC ratio. Different cash constraints, risk, margin, and retention uncertainty imply different acceptable economics.

### 12.5 Payback

A simplified subscription payback estimate is:

```text
CAC payback months = CAC / average monthly contribution per acquired customer
```

Use actual cohort contribution curves when onboarding cost, annual billing, expansion, churn, or seasonality are material. A forecasted payback is not realized cash recovery.

### 12.6 Funnel quality

Report progression with:

- counts and rates;
- eligible unit and deduplication;
- time window and maturity;
- source and campaign taxonomy;
- fit/qualification;
- activation and retained outcome;
- sales and service capacity;
- reversal, refund, and churn;
- incremental comparison where possible.

Marketing-qualified lead, sales-qualified lead, opportunity, and customer must have versioned definitions and owners.

### 12.7 Marginal versus average return

Average performance at current spend does not show the return on the next dollar. Budget decisions need:

- response curves or controlled spend tests;
- saturation and frequency;
- channel interaction;
- organic baseline and demand capture;
- capacity constraints;
- uncertainty at the proposed spend change.

Do not scale by multiplying historical average ROAS.

## 13. Experiments, incrementality, and attribution

### 13.1 Separate three questions

1. **Attribution:** Which touchpoints receive credit under a chosen rule or model?
2. **Incrementality:** What additional outcome occurred because of the intervention?
3. **Optimization:** Given uncertainty and constraints, where should the next resources go?

Attribution can support operations. It does not automatically establish causation.

### 13.2 Common attribution limits

- last click overcredits demand capture;
- first touch overcredits discovery;
- multi-touch weights can be conventional or model-dependent;
- view-through windows can claim conversions that would occur anyway;
- walled gardens can assign overlapping credit;
- identity loss and consent create missingness;
- offline, sales, PR, word-of-mouth, and brand effects are incomplete;
- channel platform reporting is interested evidence;
- branded search can capture demand created elsewhere.

Preserve raw touchpoints and business outcomes, but describe attributed conversions as attributed.

### 13.3 Incrementality designs

| Design | Suitable use | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| User/account randomized holdout | Addressable exposure and outcome | Spillover, identity, opportunity cost |
| Geo experiment | Market-level media or unavailable user randomization | Few heterogeneous geos, spillover, concurrent events |
| Switchback/time randomization | Marketplace or operations with temporal assignment | Carryover and seasonality |
| Matched market/quasi-experiment | Randomization unavailable | Residual confounding and match dependence |
| Brand lift survey experiment | Attitude and recall outcome | Survey and platform coverage bias |
| Conversion lift | Causal conversions under platform design | Eligibility, contamination, modeling, platform scope |

Google Research documents randomized geo experiments for advertising effectiveness ([Vaver and Koehler](https://research.google/pubs/measuring-ad-effectiveness-using-geo-experiments/)) and later robust methods for incremental ROAS with paired geographies ([Chen and Au](https://research.google/pubs/robust-causal-inference-for-incremental-return-on-ad-spend-with-randomized-paired-geo-experiments/)).

### 13.4 Experiment specification

Predefine:

- causal question and mechanism;
- eligible population and assignment unit;
- control and treatment;
- exposure and contamination;
- primary outcome and guardrails;
- minimum meaningful effect;
- power, sample, duration, and stopping;
- lag and maturity;
- interference and concurrent campaigns;
- missingness, invalid traffic, and telemetry;
- planned segments and multiplicity;
- scale, stop, iterate, and inconclusive rules.

Use the experiment guidance in the [product analytics reference](../../analytics/references/research.md).

### 13.5 Interpret lift reports carefully

Report:

- absolute and relative lift;
- treatment/control outcomes and counts;
- interval or posterior uncertainty;
- population, assignment unit, exposure, and dates;
- spend and opportunity cost;
- contamination and quality status;
- modeled versus directly observed components;
- practical and economic significance;
- persistence and external validity.

Google explicitly distinguishes standard attributed conversions from incremental conversions in its current Conversion Lift reporting documentation ([measurement data](https://support.google.com/google-ads/answer/14102450)). Vendor methods and eligibility change; verify the deployed version.

### 13.6 Marketing mix models

Marketing mix modeling (MMM) estimates aggregate relationships among marketing inputs, controls, and outcomes over time or geography. It can support channel interaction and budget scenarios where user-level attribution is incomplete.

MMM requires:

- sufficient variation and history;
- correct outcome and spend/exposure data;
- controls for trend, seasonality, price, promotions, distribution, economy, product, and competitors;
- transformations for lag and saturation;
- priors and assumptions disclosed;
- holdout and predictive checks;
- uncertainty and sensitivity;
- calibration against experiments where possible;
- scenario constraints and marginal response.

Google's open-source [Meridian](https://developers.google.com/meridian) documentation emphasizes model transparency and experiment calibration. A model can fit history and still assign causal credit incorrectly if inputs are collinear or confounders are absent.

### 13.7 Build a measurement portfolio

Use:

- always-on delivery and outcome analytics;
- controlled lift tests for causal calibration;
- MMM for aggregate allocation and response curves;
- brand tracking for memory and meaning;
- cohort economics for customer quality;
- qualitative research for mechanism;
- operational and sales evidence for feasibility.

Reconcile, do not force agreement. Different methods cover different populations, periods, and constructs.

## 14. Evidence standards for public claims

### 14.1 Substantiate before publication

The U.S. FTC's [Advertising Substantiation Policy Statement](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/ftc-policy-statement-regarding-advertising-substantiation) states that advertisers need a reasonable basis for objective express and implied claims before dissemination. The required evidence depends on the claim, product, consequence of error, benefit of truth, substantiation cost, and what relevant experts consider reasonable.

This is a U.S. legal source, not a global rule. The durable operational standard is:

> Identify every reasonable material interpretation of the complete ad and possess fit-for-purpose evidence for each objective claim before it is published.

### 14.2 Evaluate the net impression

Meaning comes from the full communication:

- words and omissions;
- image, demonstration, chart, sound, and motion;
- headline, label, and CTA;
- proximity and prominence of qualifications;
- audience, channel, device, and timing;
- landing page and surrounding content;
- endorsement or publisher context.

The FTC's [Native Advertising Guide](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/native-advertising-guide-businesses) describes evaluating the net impression and making commercial content identifiable before people engage with it.

### 14.3 Claim evidence matrix

| Claim type | Minimum evidence packet | Common failure |
|---|---|---|
| Current fact | Authoritative source, version, date, scope | Stale or inconsistent page |
| Capability | Reproducible product evidence under stated conditions | Roadmap described as available |
| Numeric outcome | Method, population, baseline, denominator, period, uncertainty | Decorative percentage |
| Comparative | Same criteria, relevant versions, fair setup, date | Cherry-picked feature or old competitor |
| Superlative | Defined universe, measure, geography, period, exhaustive-enough search | Undefined “best” or “#1” |
| Customer result | Verified record, permission, context, typicality boundary | Treating case as expected result |
| Scientific/health/safety | Evidence level appropriate to domain and claim; qualified review | Anecdote or mechanistic speculation |
| Security/privacy | Technical evidence, scope, threat/model, owner, current version | “Secure” without defined control |
| Sustainability | Defined boundary, lifecycle/method, current data, qualified review | Vague green claim |
| Forecast/estimate | Model, inputs, assumptions, scenarios, owner | Target presented as prediction |
| Guarantee | Conditions, exclusions, remedy, operational ability | Headline promise narrowed in fine print |

### 14.4 Maintain a claim register

```yaml
claim_id: CL-001
exact_claim: "..."
claim_type: fact | capability | outcome | comparative | estimate | opinion
reasonable_interpretations:
  - "..."
audience_and_channels:
evidence_sources:
method_and_locator:
scope_and_limitations:
required_disclosure:
product_version:
owner:
reviewers:
approved_at:
expires_or_recheck_at:
withdrawal_trigger:
downstream_assets:
```

Tie the claim ID to ads, pages, decks, scripts, feeds, partner kits, and sales materials so a changed fact can be withdrawn everywhere.

### 14.5 Disclosures do not rescue a contradictory headline

Necessary qualifications should be:

- close to the claim;
- noticeable and understandable;
- available on every relevant device and format;
- present before a decision;
- in the language of the message;
- durable enough to perceive;
- repeated where content can be separated or shared.

FTC [.com Disclosures](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/com-disclosures-how-make-effective-disclosures-digital-advertising) describes proximity, prominence, device, format, and comprehension considerations. If the truthful qualification destroys the main promise, rewrite the promise.

### 14.6 Testimonials, endorsements, influencers, and reviews

- verify the person and experience;
- preserve their actual meaning and current view;
- substantiate objective claims as if the advertiser made them;
- disclose material connections clearly;
- obtain permission for name, quote, logo, image, and context;
- state generally expected results where required;
- do not suppress, condition, buy, or fabricate reviews;
- do not use employees or insiders as apparently independent reviewers;
- monitor partners and creators acting on the organization's behalf.

The FTC's [Endorsement Guides Q&A](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftcs-endorsement-guides-what-people-are-asking) reflects the guides revised in 2023. The [Consumer Reviews and Testimonials Rule Q&A](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/consumer-reviews-testimonials-rule-questions-answers) explains a U.S. rule effective in 2024 that addresses fake reviews, undisclosed insider reviews, review suppression, and related practices.

A testimonial is evidence of one reported experience. It is not scientific or population-level substantiation.

### 14.7 Comparative claims

Define:

- compared product, tier, version, and date;
- target use case and population;
- criteria and weights;
- setup, inputs, hardware, data, or service conditions;
- price basis and included costs;
- measurement and uncertainty;
- party conducting and funding the comparison;
- material competitor strengths and limitations.

The FTC's [comparative advertising policy](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/statement-policy-regarding-comparative-advertising) supports truthful, nondeceptive comparisons on objectively measurable attributes or price. Do not make an apparently objective comparison from subjective or undisclosed criteria.

### 14.8 Market and customer numbers

For “X% of leaders,” “three times faster,” “$5B market,” or similar claims, publish or preserve:

- exact question or metric;
- numerator, denominator, and unit;
- population and sample;
- recruitment and field dates;
- comparison and baseline;
- weighting and exclusions;
- sponsor and method;
- uncertainty and rounding;
- source link and date;
- whether observation, estimate, forecast, or target.

Do not create authority through survey theatre. AAPOR's [disclosure standards](https://aapor.org/standards-and-ethics/disclosure-standards/) require enough methodological information for independent review of survey claims.

### 14.9 Regulated and high-risk categories

Health, finance, housing, employment, education, children, safety, security, privacy, environment, and legal services may require different evidence and review. Establish:

- regulator and jurisdiction;
- applicable product classification;
- prohibited and required claims;
- evidence standard and qualified expert;
- audience vulnerability;
- disclosure and record-retention requirements;
- adverse-event or complaint monitoring;
- escalation and withdrawal process.

Do not generalize a legal review from one market to another.

## 15. Marketing operations, governance, and maintenance

### 15.1 Assign decision rights

| Decision | Accountable owner | Required input |
|---|---|---|
| Market/ICP | Business/product leader | Research, sales, success, finance, product |
| Positioning | Marketing/product | Customer evidence, competitive truth, leadership |
| Product claim | Domain/product owner | Evidence owner, legal/regulatory where needed |
| Campaign | Campaign owner | Brand, channel, product, operations, measurement |
| Lifecycle | Lifecycle/product owner | Consent/privacy, product state, support |
| Brand tracker | Insights owner | Sampling/method expert, market owner |
| Measurement | Analytics/finance owner | Marketing, data, platform, privacy |
| Publication | Named approver | Claim, accessibility, legal, brand, channel review |

“Marketing approved it” is not sufficient when the claim depends on security, science, pricing, or customer operations.

### 15.2 Maintain controlled systems

- audience and ICP definitions;
- category and positioning decision;
- message hierarchy and approved terminology;
- claim and disclosure register;
- asset, license, permission, and testimonial records;
- campaign, channel, creative, offer, and UTM taxonomy;
- consent, preference, and suppression rules;
- metric semantic layer and source lineage;
- experiment and learning archive;
- content, feed, sales, and partner distribution inventory;
- review dates and retirement status.

### 15.3 Review by risk

**Low risk:** routine factual content with stable approved claims.

**Moderate:** new campaign, comparison, pricing, customer story, new audience, or channel.

**High:** regulated topic, safety, health, finance, privacy, environmental, security, children, material guarantee, new market law, or irreversible brand event.

High-risk content needs qualified review and stronger substantiation, not merely additional approvers.

### 15.4 Quality assurance before launch

- product, price, terms, availability, and CTA are true;
- claims map to current evidence and allowed scope;
- disclosures survive format, crop, audio, localization, and device;
- links, forms, routing, codes, billing, and confirmation work;
- analytics taxonomy, consent, and experiment assignment are verified;
- accessibility works across content and creative;
- suppression and preferences are respected;
- landing and lifecycle follow-through match the promise;
- inventory, sales, support, and incident owners are ready;
- rollback and claim withdrawal are possible.

### 15.5 Preserve negative results and learning

Archive:

- failed or inconclusive campaigns;
- poor-fit segments;
- messages people misunderstood;
- claims that could not be substantiated;
- tests invalidated by tracking or contamination;
- tactics that lifted response but harmed quality;
- retired assets and why;
- changes to definitions and series.

This prevents repeated waste and favorable-result bias.

### 15.6 Maintenance triggers

Re-review when:

- product, price, terms, policy, or availability changes;
- evidence expires or is corrected;
- a competitor changes the comparison;
- a regulation or platform policy changes;
- a new market or language launches;
- a complaint, incident, or accessibility defect challenges the claim;
- campaign audience or format changes the net impression;
- measurement method or vendor changes;
- the owner leaves or the asset becomes orphaned.

### 16.9 Marketing definition of done

- The customer decision, market boundary, owner, and consequence of error are explicit.
- ICP and disqualification reflect product outcome, readiness, cost to serve, and evidence—not stereotypes or aspiration.
- Positioning names the real alternative, mechanism, proof, and tradeoff.
- Messaging is accurate, understandable, relevant, distinctive, credible, and accessible.
- Offer, price, terms, renewal, cancellation, and required effort are visible before commitment.
- GTM motion matches buying complexity and operational capacity.
- Campaign channel roles, reach, frequency, creative, handoffs, and stop rules are defined.
- Lifecycle messages use valid state, purpose, preference, timing, suppression, and exit logic.
- Brand measures distinguish awareness, associations, choice, experience, behavior, and monetary valuation.
- Delivery, attribution, incrementality, economics, and long-term value remain distinct.
- Public claims have prior substantiation, claim-level provenance, qualified disclosures, owners, expiry, and withdrawal paths.
- Testimonials, reviews, customer logos, endorsements, media, and data have genuine permission and provenance.
- Applicable legal, privacy, accessibility, platform, and regulated-domain reviews are current.
- Tracking, consent, landing, routing, product, billing, support, and rollback were verified.
- Results show baselines, counts, definitions, uncertainty, guardrails, contradictions, and mature outcomes.
- Negative and inconclusive evidence is retained.
- No fabricated customer, quote, market size, certification, urgency, price, capability, result, review, or confidence remains.
